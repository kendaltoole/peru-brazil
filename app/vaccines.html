Health & Safety

Vaccine & Health Guidance

What you need to know before traveling with a 3-year-old

Important Disclaimer This page is informational guidance, not medical advice. Always consult a travel clinic or your pediatrician for personalized recommendations based on your family's health history and current conditions.

Recommended Timeline (April Trip)

  • 6–8 weeks before departure: Visit a travel clinic. This gives time for multi-dose vaccines and malaria prophylaxis planning.
  • At minimum 10+ days before: Get the yellow fever shot to clear the validity window for your certificate.
  • 1–2 weeks before: Fill any prescriptions (malaria meds, emergency supplies) and prepare bug protection gear.

Critical Vaccines

Yellow Fever Critical Amazon

The big one for Manaus/Amazon

Yellow fever is a mosquito-borne disease, and vaccination is the primary protection when traveling to the Amazon region (including Manaus).

  • Many countries (and sometimes airlines/routes) require proof of yellow fever vaccination if you've been in a risk area
  • The vaccine must be given at least 10 days before travel for the certificate to be valid
  • Age requirement: generally allowed from 9 months+ (your 3-year-old qualifies)
Action: Schedule this vaccine early in your prep timeline to ensure the 10-day validity window is met before your Brazil leg.

Recommended Vaccines

Hepatitis A Recommended Peru & Brazil

Food & water exposure protection

Hepatitis A is commonly recommended for travel where food and water exposure risk is higher. This applies to both Peru and Brazil, especially if eating outside major hotels or in smaller towns/lodges.

Note: Check if your child has already received Hep A as part of routine childhood vaccines—it's often given between 12-23 months.

Typhoid Recommended Peru & Brazil

Food & water-borne illness

Typhoid vaccination is frequently recommended for travelers to parts of Peru and Brazil, especially if you'll eat outside major hotels or in smaller towns/lodges (which you will at the jungle lodge).

Routine Childhood Vaccines Verify General

Quick sanity check before international travel

Before international travel, make sure routine vaccines are up to date:

  • MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella)
  • DTaP (Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis)
  • Polio
  • Varicella (Chickenpox)
  • Flu (seasonal)

Situational Considerations

Rabies Situational Amazon

Worth considering for Amazon wildlife exposure

Not everyone needs pre-exposure rabies vaccination, but it's more relevant if:

  • You'll be around animals or bats
  • Your lodge has lots of wildlife encounters
  • You want extra safety margin for a child (kids may not clearly report a bite or scratch)
Even without pre-vaccination: Know where the nearest facility is that can provide post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP). Your lodge should have this information.

Malaria Prevention

Understanding Malaria Risk by Region

Cusco/Machu Picchu (Week 1): Malaria risk is generally not a concern at those elevations. You don't need to worry about prophylaxis for the Peru leg.

Manaus/Amazon (Week 2): Malaria risk exists in many parts of the Amazon. The approach is two-fold:

  • Strong mosquito bite prevention (see below)
  • Consider malaria prophylaxis depending on exact lodge location and activities
For Your 3-Year-Old Malaria prophylaxis is possible for children, but the medication choice and dosage are weight- and itinerary-dependent. A travel clinic or pediatrician needs to evaluate and prescribe the right option.

Other Mosquito-Borne Illnesses

Dengue, Zika & Others

There are no routine vaccines for most travelers for these diseases. Prevention is primarily through bite avoidance.

Dengue: Present in Brazil. Prevention = bite avoidance.

Zika: Can be a consideration, particularly for pregnancy planning. Check current advisories closer to your travel date as guidance can change by location.

Kid-Friendly Bug Prevention (Amazon Week)

The Single Biggest Quality-of-Life Factor

For the Amazon week, your mosquito bite prevention strategy is critical—both for comfort and disease prevention.

  • Child-safe insect repellent – Look for DEET-based products approved for children (typically 10-30% DEET for kids). Picaridin is another effective option.
  • Permethrin-treated clothing – Treat or buy pre-treated long sleeves, pants, and socks
  • Bug net for sleeping – Most lodges provide these, but confirm in advance
  • Long, light-colored clothing – Covers skin while staying cool
  • Avoid peak mosquito hours – Dawn and dusk are worst
  • Air conditioning when available – Mosquitoes are less active in cooled rooms
Pro tip: Apply repellent to your own hands first, then rub onto your child's exposed skin. Avoid hands, eyes, and mouth. Reapply after swimming or heavy sweating.

Pre-Trip Health Checklist

  • Schedule travel clinic appointment (6-8 weeks before)
  • Yellow fever vaccine (at least 10 days before Brazil)
  • Hepatitis A vaccine (if not already current)
  • Typhoid vaccine
  • Verify routine childhood vaccines are up to date
  • Discuss malaria prophylaxis for Amazon week
  • Discuss rabies pre-exposure vaccine (optional)
  • Get prescriptions filled (malaria meds if recommended)
  • Purchase child-safe insect repellent (DEET or Picaridin)
  • Treat clothing with permethrin or buy pre-treated items
  • Pack a basic first-aid kit with pediatric medications
